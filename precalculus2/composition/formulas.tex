Algebraically, formulas for compositions are formed through replacement.





\begin{idea} \textbf{\textcolor{blue!55!black}{Composition}}  


Let $f$ be a function with domain $D_f$.


Let $g$ be a function with domain $D_g$.




Then the \textbf{\textcolor{green!50!black}{composition of f and g, $f \circ g$,}} is defined as

\[
(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x))
\] 


The value of $g$ becomes a domain number for $f$.


The composition is defined on a subset of the domain of $g$.  The composition is defined at those numbers in the domain of $g$ where the value of $g$ is in the domain of $f$.




\end{idea}





Algebraically, $f(g(x))$ is our map. 


$f(g(x))$ tells us to replace all occurrences of the variable in the formula for $f$ with the whole formula for $g$. 




\begin{example} Composition Domain


Let $f(k) = (k + 5)(k - 3)$ with its natural domain.


Let $g(t) = \sqrt{3t}$ with its natural domain.





We would like a formula for $f \circ g$. 

\textbf{\textcolor{red!75!green}{explanation}} 

$(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x))$ 

This tells us to replace all occurrences of the variable in the formula for $f$ with the whole formula for $g$.  There are two instances of the variable $k$ in the formula for $f$.  These should be replaced with $\sqrt{3t}$. We just have to remember to use the correct variable for $(f \circ g)(x)$. 



\[
(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)) = (\sqrt{3x} + 5)(\sqrt{3x} - 3)
\]






We would like a formula for $g \circ f$. 

\textbf{\textcolor{red!75!green}{explanation}} 

$(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x))$ 

This tells us to replace all occurrences of the variable in the formula for $g$ with the whole formula for $f$.  There is only one instance of the variable $t$ in the formula for $g$.  It should be replaced with $(k + 5)(k - 3)$. We just have to remember to use the correct variable for $(g \circ f)(x)$. 



\[
(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x)) = \sqrt{3(x + 5)(x - 3)}
\]


\end{example}






\begin{observation} \textbf{\textcolor{-red!75}{Domains}}


We must always pay attention to domains.  Here the natural domain of $g$ is $[0, \infty)$ and the range of $g$ is $[0, \infty)$.  The range of $g$ fits totally inside the natural domain of $f$, which is $(-\infty, \infty)$.  Therefore, we can use the whole domain of $g$ as the domain of $(f \circ g)$.

This is not the case for $(g \circ f)(x)$.


The natural range of $f$ is $[-16, 8)$ contains negative numbers, which are not in the domain of $g$.

In the composition, we cannot use domain numbers of $f$, for which $f$ has a negative value.

We cannot use $(-5, 3)$.

The domain of $(g \circ f)$ is $(-\infty, -5] \cup [3, \infty)$.


\end{observation}













\begin{example} Composition Domain


Let $f(k) = \ln(k - 4)$ with its natural domain.


Let $g(t) = \frac{1}{t}$ with its natural domain.



\textbf{\textcolor{red!75!green}{explanation}} 

We would like a formula for $f \circ g$. 

$(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x))$ 

This tells us to replace all occurrences of the variable in the formula for $f$ with the whole formula for $g$.  There is only one instance of the variable $k$ in the formula for $f$.  This should be replaced with $\frac{1}{t}$. We just have to remember to use the correct variable for $(f \circ g)(x)$. 



\[
(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)) = \ln\left( \frac{1}{t} - 4 \right)
\]








\begin{observation} \textbf{\textcolor{-red!75}{Domain}}


We must always pay attention to domains.  Here the domain of $g$ is $(-\infty, 0) \cup (0, \infty)$ and the range of $g$ is $(-\infty, 0) \cup (0, \infty)$.  This range does not fit totally inside the domain of $f$, which is $(4, \infty)$.  We cannot have negative values of $g$ less than $4$.  \\


We need $\frac{1}{t} > 4$

Therefore, we need to restrict the domain of $g$ to $\left(0, \frac{1}{4} \right)$.



Therefore, the domain of $(f \circ g)$ is $\left(0, \frac{1}{4} \right)$.

\end{observation}



\end{example}



















\begin{example} Composition Domain


Let $f(k) = \ln(k - 4)$ with its natural domain.


Let $g(t) = \frac{1}{t}$ with its natural domain.



\textbf{\textcolor{red!75!green}{explanation}} 

We would like a formula for $g \circ f$. 

$(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x))$ 

This tells us to replace all occurrences of the variable in the formula for $g$ with the whole formula for $f$.  There is only one instance of the variable $t$ in the formula for $g$.  This should be replaced with $\ln(k - 4)$. We just have to remember to use the correct variable for $(g \circ f)(x)$. 



\[
(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x)) =  \frac{1}{\ln(x - 4)}
\]








\begin{observation} \textbf{\textcolor{-red!75}{Domain}}


We must always pay attention to domains.  Here the domain of $f$ is $(4, \infty)$ and the range of $f$ is $(-\infty, \infty)$.  This range does not fit totally inside the domain of $g$, which is $(-\infty, 0) \cup (0, \infty)$.  We cannot have $0$.  


WWhere does $f(k) = \ln(k - 4) = 0$? At $5$.

We need to remove $5$ from the domain of $(g \circ f)$.

We need to restrict the domain of $f$ to $(4, 5) \cup (5, \infty)$.



Therefore, the domain of $(g \circ f)$ is $(4, 5) \cup (5, \infty)$.

\end{observation}



\end{example}

















\begin{example} Composition Components


Let $H(x) = 5 \ln(\sqrt{x^2-3})$ with its natural domain. 


We would like to view this as a composition. 



\textbf{\textcolor{red!75!green}{explanation}} 


We are looking for functions $f$ and $g$ such that $(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)) = 5 \ln(\sqrt{x^2-3})$ 


First, identify ``insides'' of functions.  In this case, $x^2 - 3$ is the inside of $5 \ln(\sqrt{x})$. 




Let $f(t) = 5 \ln(\sqrt{t})$. 

Let $g(y) = y^2 - 3$. 


Algebraically, this produces the formula want, $(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x)) = 5 \ln(\sqrt{x^2-3})$.










\begin{observation} \textbf{\textcolor{-red!75}{Domain}}

Our formula presents two problems.

First, the inside of the square root must be $0$ of positive.

Second, the inside of the logarithm must be positive. 



So, we need $x^2 - 3 \geq 0 $, which means $x \in (-\infty, -\sqrt{3}] \cup [\sqrt{3}, \infty)$. 


However, this domain for $g$ will produce $0$ as a function value for $g$, which cannot go into $f$.  SO, we need to remove $-\sqrt{3}$ and $\sqrt{3}$.




The domain of $f \circ g$ is $(-\infty, -\sqrt{3}) \cup (\sqrt{3}, \infty)$.




\end{observation}


\end{example}
